Inferential Analysis

Descriptive statistics are used to summarise and visualise large datasets, which in turn facilitates simpler interpretation and analysis thereof – this is where inferential statistics is utilised. Unlike descriptive statistics, inferential statistics are used to make predictions (“inferences”) or generalisations about the same dataset. Boslaugh (2012) describes it as the process of characterising or making decisions about a population via the information gathered from a sample that was extracted from the said population. Consequently, it is important to ensure that the sample is an accurate representation of the population being accessed (Laerd, 2018).

Sampling strategies are used to achieve this with the two major groups being probability and non-probability sampling techniques. Sample accuracy and quality have a considerable impact on the predictions derived for the population being analysed. Considering sampling by nature incurs sampling errors, it is expected for a sample to be a flawed representation of the population. This ultimately gave way to inferential statistics (Laerd, 2018).

The term “inference” is defined by the Merriam-Webster (2017) online dictionary as, “the act of passing from statistical sample data to generalizations (as of the value of population parameters) usually with calculated degrees of certainty”. According to Boslaugh (2012) inference is a process of making judgments relating to the unknown, drawing on what is at present known to be true. In other words, inferential statistics is a particular type of inference in which judgments are made about a population, as previously stated. The two central areas of inferential statistics are:
  1. The estimation of parameters (using sample statistics such as the sample mean to describe the population mean) and
  2. testing of statistical hypotheses (using sample data to answer research questions such as, is it valid to assume that the data follows a normal distribution? Or whether people who sleep more live longer?)
Various models and methods exist that are used in inferential statistics such as the binomial theorem, normal distributions, t-distributions, hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, central limit theorem, and linear regression, just to name a few (Glen, 2014). These models and methods in conjunction with the numerous descriptive statistical measures form the basis of most research analysis.
