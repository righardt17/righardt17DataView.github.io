Descriptive Analysis

At the foundation of data analysis and the understanding of data lies descriptive statistics. It is the most rudimentary and widespread use of data in organisations today (Gibson, no date). It provides a general understanding of the distribution of the data, helps identify outliers and errors, as well as enabling the determination of relationships among variables (Dhand, 2015).

Descriptive statistics helps to summarise and visualise large sets of data which enables it to be presented in a more meaningful way. This allows for simpler interpretations and analysis of the data. However, descriptive statistics do not allow for conclusions to be made or generalisations that extend beyond the data. It merely describes the data and what it represents (Laerd, 2018).

After grouping and organising the data, various statistical measures are employed to characterise it. The following statistical measures of descriptive analysis may be utilised:
  • Measures of central tendency.
  • Measures of divergence from normality.
  • Measures of spread.
These basic measures lay the foundation for further statistical testing, that are of increasing complexity, and will be used to derive conclusions (Shodganga, 2008).

Measures of Central Tendency

Central tendency is a descriptive summary of a dataset by way of a single value that indicates the centre of the data distribution. It provides a comprehensive summary of the whole dataset but yields no information concerning the individual values of the dataset (Gooch, 2011).

The mean, median and mode are the three main measures used to describe the central tendency of a dataset. Under certain conditions, some measures are more suitable to use than others. This section will indicate how these three measures are calculated and describe under what conditions they are most applicable (Lund Research, 2013).

The mean is the most well-known measure of central tendency. Although it is mostly used with continuous data, it may also be used with discrete data as well. It denotes the sum of all the values in the data set divided by the total number of values present in the dataset. The mean of a dataset may be calculated using only a sample of the total population, called the sample mean, or by using all the values in the dataset, referred to as the population mean. Another property of the mean is that it minimises the error involved in predicting any value in the dataset. Furthermore, it is the only measure of central tendency where the sum of the deviations of each value in the dataset from the mean value is always zero.

Although regarded by many as the best measure of central tendency for quantitative data, the mean has one major disadvantage, it is particularly sensitive to the influence of outliers in the dataset (Lund Research, 2013). Thus, extreme values may distort the mean and therefore lead to it being disregarded as an accurate representation of the dataset. The mode is the value in a dataset with the highest frequency of occurrence and is the only measure of central tendency that may be applied to categorical variables. A dataset may potentially have more than one mode, and also have none if all the categories are distinct (Kumar, 2018). On a histogram, it is depicted by the highest frequency value and therefore may be acknowledged as the most favoured option as seen in Figure 5.

In the case of two or more modes, it generates indecisiveness as to which mode best describes the central tendency of the data. This is especially problematic when working with continuous data since it is more inclined to consist of individual data values and not exhibit values with higher frequencies than others. Small continuous datasets with a large range will likely not produce a mode, while the opposite will have a higher probability of displaying one. Therefore, the mode is seldomly used with continuous data (Lund Research, 2013).

Another problem with the mode arises when the most common data point exists far from the other data points in the set. An example of such an occurrence is shown in Figure 8, where the mode of 2 is not representative of the data. The data predominately lies within the 20 to 30 value range. In this case, it would be misleading to use the mode to describe the central tendency of the dataset.

In any symmetrical distribution, the mean, median and mode have the same value and are located at the centre of the distribution. However, in such a case, the mean is widely considered to be the optimum measure of central tendency because it includes all the values in the dataset for its calculation. The mean is also the only measure to be affected by the change in any given value in the dataset (Lund Research, 2013).

However, when the data distribution is skewed, as seen in the right-skewed dataset of Figure 10, the mean is also pulled in the direction of the skew. Under such circumstances, the median is generally considered to be the most accurate measure of central tendency. As the distribution becomes more skewed, the disparity between the mean and median increases, as well as the emphasis placed on using the median instead of the mean (Lund Research, 2013).

Measures of Divergence from Normality

One of the fundamental tasks of statistical analysis is to characterise the skewness and kurtosis of a dataset. A graphical technique, such as the histogram, is an effective way to visualise the skewness and kurtosis (Na, 2014). These two characteristics are used to test the normality, which indicates whether a dataset is modelled for normal distribution (Help.gooddata.com, 2007).

Skewness allows for the determination of the size in deviation of a distribution’s shape from the symmetrical structure of a normal distribution. In other words, skewness measures the lack of symmetry. A dataset is symmetrical if the left and right side of the distribution, from the centre point (mean), appears almost identical (Na, 2014). A skewness of zero denotes a perfectly symmetrical distribution since it replicates the shape of a normal distribution. It is important to note that distributions with a skewness value of zero are not always symmetrical (Date, 2019), as seen with the histogram below:

Figure 12: Example of an asymmetric distribution with a skewness value of zero (Source: Henry, 2016).

The skewness values can be interpreted with the following rule of thumb:
  • If the skewness is less than -1 or greater than 1, the data are highly skewed.
  • If the skewness is between -1 and -0.5 or between 0.5 and 1, the data are moderately skewed.
  • If the skewness is between -0.5 and 0.5, the data are fairly symmetrical (McNeese, 2008).
Skewness can also be generally described with the distribution’s mean, mode and median values. In the case where the mean, median and mode are equal (mean = median = mode), the distribution is perfectly normal. If the mean is less than the median and the median less than the mode (mean < median < mode), the distribution is negatively skewed. While when the mean is greater than the median and the median is greater than the mode (mean > median > mode), the distribution is positively skewed (Delaitre, 2011). This type of kurtosis is recognised as a platykurtic distribution, which reveals that a distribution has flat tails (small outliers). This distribution is more sensible for investors compared to a leptokurtic distribution (CFI, no date).

Measures of Spread

Measures of spread depict the similarity or variance in a sample or population. It provides an overall description of a dataset by working in combination with a measure of central tendency, such as the mode, median or mean. For example, it equips us with a better understanding of how well the mean represents that dataset. It is unfavourable for a dataset to contain a large spread of values since it indicates a large variation between individual data values. This reduces the mean’s ability to accurately represent the data. Furthermore, a small disparity between separate data groups is often preferred in research as it indicates similarity (Leard statistics, 2018).

The most well-known measures of spread are range, interquartile range (IQR), absolute deviation, variance and standard deviation.

The range is the simplest measure of spread and describes the difference between the highest and lowest values in a dataset (maximum value – minimum value). It is used to set the boundaries of a dataset to determine whether a value crosses the critically low or high threshold, which is commonly used to detect errors when entering data (Leard statistics, 2018).

Quartiles divide the dataset into quarters, similar to the way the median breaks it in half, and are used to analyse the spread of the data. Figure 16 illustrates the statistical distribution between upper quartiles (75%) – Q3 and lower quartiles (25%) – Q1 as well as the median (50%) – Q2.

Figure 16: Measure of statistical dispersion via interquartile range adapted from (Source: Kumar, 2018).

Quartiles are much less affected by outliers or a skewed dataset than other measures of spread such as the mean and standard deviation. Therefore, it is often reported that Quartiles, along with the median, are the preferred measures of spread and central tendency under these circumstances. An established way of expressing quartiles is known as an interquartile range. This range describes the centre-half of the values in the distribution and is calculated by determining the difference between the third quartile (Q3) and the first quartile (Q1) as depicted in Figure 16.

Although quartiles are considered to be a good measure of spread, they are fairly limited considering that they do not take into account all the values in the dataset. To compensate for this lack of comprehensiveness it is suggested to determine the absolute deviation, variation and standard deviation since these measures of spread incorporate all the data values and are thus more representative of the data spread (Leard statistics, 2018).

The absolute deviation is perhaps the most rudimentary form of calculating the deviation of a data value from the mean of a dataset. It involves subtracting the mean from each data value, where data values below the mean have a negative deviation and values above the mean a positive deviation.

The variance is particularly helpful with measuring the spread of data. If the data values of a dataset are broadly dispersed, the variance value will be large. Conversely, the variance value will be small if the data values are distributed within close proximity around the mean. There are however potential complications that come with using the variance. The first problem is caused by the deviations of data values from the mean being squared. This places a larger weight on extreme values. As a result outliers in the dataset can drastically increase the variation and it is therefore considered good practice to remove outliers from a dataset to garner a better representation and understanding when analysing it.

This prohibits it from being placed within frequency distributions and cannot be directly associated with the values from the dataset (Leard statistics, 2018).
